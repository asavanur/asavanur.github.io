We'll now take a detour into Ancient Egypt, at around the same time. The Ancient Egyptians also had a high level of science and math, though more is known about the Babylonians. <!--more--> Documents are more scarce given that they were written on papyrus, which is of course much more fragile than a clay tablet.

To create their massive pyramids and other structures, the Egyptians needed to gather a large amount of workers. This naturally led to the problems of how to distribute food and payment. Smaller-scale problems of distribution arose in temples and other such organizations.

People were also paid in beer and bread. They didn't get drunk all the time, their beer varied from quite strong to quite weak, but the beer used as a form of payment was nutritious enough to be a staple{%sidenote 'One' 'M. Bunson. The encyclopedia of Ancient Egypt (GramercyBooks, 1991), p.5' %}.

Not everyone was paid equally. The priests in the temple were paid more than the security guards, for example, and the factor by which they were paid more was fixed. The question of how to distribute a given quantity of food thus became more than a division problem. It became a linear equation, one of the first instances of what we now call 'algebra'. 

There is a papyrus account of the wages paid to the various employees of a certain funerary temple. This papyrus lists 13 different jobs, each with their own wage multiplication factor. The distribution problem for this temple would be very lengthy to write out. So we will consider a simpler problem that illustrates the same ideas. (The real-world problems encountered by the mathematician/accountants were often harder than the ones that they were trained on!{%sidenote 'Two' 'A. Imhausen, Mathematics in Ancient Egypt: A Contextual History (Princeton University Press, 2016). p.107-109.'%})

Here is our simplified problem: Suppose that you have 15 slices of bread to divide among two people. But one of them worked for twice as much time as the other one. Remember that bread is wages, so how many slices does each one get?
To write the equation corresponding to this problem, remember that the slice amounts have to add up to 15. We can call the smaller amount 'x'. Then the statement "two times the small amount combined with the same small amount is 15" is, in symbols, $$2x+x=15.$$

The Egyptians solved problems of this type with two methods, "false position"{%sidenote 'Three' 'See the last footnote in the previous blog post for a related comment. One of the procedures they used is equivalent to false position. A. Imhausen, Mathematics in Ancient Egypt: A Contextual History (Princeton University Press, 2016). p.71.'%} and exact solution.
False position is also called false assumption. In a modified way, it survives today when solving complicated equations on a computer. It consists of making an initial guess and then modifying it to correct for the error. In this example, we can choose an initial guess of 1 slice (it doesn't matter that much what the exact value is). Then try to see if it works: $$2 \times 1+1=3,$$ not 15, so this is too small. But we can multiply this wrong guess by a factor to correct it. 15/3 is 5, so try multiplying 1 by 5. We get 5, which works. The small amount is 5 slices, and the large amount is 10. 
 
They also had another method to do this: the exact solution. 
Simply consider the person who takes twice the small amount as 'two people', and divide the slices equally. There are three total 'people' now, so 15/3=5. That is the amount for one person, i.e. the smaller amount. Multiply by 2 to get the amount for 'two people', and you get 10. 

We now have two ways of solving an equation without symbol manipulation.
